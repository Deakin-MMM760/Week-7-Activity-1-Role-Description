MMM760 | Week 7

Activity 1 | Role Description

Design a role your practice business needs to fill. Define it properly before writing a word of an advertisement. A role that is unclear before recruitment begins stays unclear all the way through to performance management.

What you're producing

A role purpose statement in one or two sentences.

Three to five core responsibilities — the main things this person does regularly.

Two or three essential selection criteria — what they genuinely must have to do the job.

One or two desirable criteria — useful but not required.

A working context description covering reporting line, team, and typical working environment.

A brief reflection on what defining the role reveals about where the business is headed.

1. Set the context

Name the business and identify the role. Be as specific as possible — "Customer Service Person" is not a role title. "Client Onboarding Coordinator" is.

Is the owner overwhelmed? Has demand grown beyond what the current team can handle? Is there a capability gap?

2. Role purpose

Write a purpose statement in one or two sentences. A strong purpose statement tells someone why this role exists — not just what it does.

The purpose test

If you removed this role, what problem would go unsolved or what opportunity would go unrealised? The answer is the purpose.

One to two sentences. Start with the outcome, not the tasks.

3. Core responsibilities

List three to five core responsibilities — the main things this person is accountable for on a regular basis. Each responsibility should be a complete action, not a task list. Write at the level of outcomes, not activities.

Outcome versus activity

"Answers the phone" is an activity. "Manages all inbound client enquiries and ensures every lead receives a response within four hours" is a responsibility with an accountable outcome built in.

4. Selection criteria

Separate what is genuinely required to do the job from what would be nice to have. Overstating essential criteria shrinks the candidate pool unnecessarily.

Essential versus desirable

Ask: could a highly capable person without this attribute still perform the role effectively? If yes, it is desirable at most — not essential.

Essential criterion 1

A skill, qualification, or experience the candidate must have to do the job.

Essential criterion 2

A skill, qualification, or experience the candidate must have to do the job.

Essential criterion 3 (optional)

A third essential criterion if genuinely required by the role.

One or two attributes that would be a bonus but are not required. Keep this list short — long desirable lists function as hidden essential criteria.

5. Working context

Describe the working environment clearly. A candidate who understands the context before they accept the role is more likely to stay and perform well.

Who does this person report to? Who do they work alongside day-to-day?

Where is the work done? What are the hours? Is there flexibility?

Including the range signals transparency and prevents wasted time for both parties. Check the relevant Modern Award rate on the Fair Work website if unsure of the minimum.

6. Reflection

Complete this after finishing the role description.

Two to three sentences. Focus on what the act of designing the role reveals — not just a description of what you wrote.

Quality check

Is the role title specific enough that the right candidate would recognise themselves in it?

Does the purpose statement describe an outcome, not a task list?

Would two different people interviewing for this role ask the same questions and assess the answers the same way?

Have you removed any essential criteria that a capable person could acquire on the job?

Does the working context section give a candidate enough information to decide whether the role suits them before they apply?
